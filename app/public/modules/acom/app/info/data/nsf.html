National Science Foundation

All NSF proposals with a due date of January 15, 2011 or later must include a Data Management Plan, no longer than two pages, that describes the PI’s plans for managing data and sharing the products of research, or shows that such plans are not needed.

Your Data Management Plan should describe how the project will conform to NSF policy on disseminating and sharing research results, including:

  • the types of data, samples, physical collections, software, curriculum materials, and other materials that will be produced during the project
  • the standards that will be used for the format and content of data and metadata (if existing standards are absent or deemed inadequate, document the situation and propose solutions or remedies)
  • policies for access and sharing, including provisions for appropriate protection of privacy, confidentiality, security, intellectual property, or other rights or requirements
  • policies and provisions for re-using and re-distributing data, and for producing derivatives
  • plans for archiving data, samples, and other research products and for preserving access to them

If there’s room, include a statement of institutional procedures and capabilities for data storage and maintenance. Your plan is the most important information; if it fills the two pages, you can put the institutional information in the Project Description, as part of the Intellectual Merit or Broader Impacts. See Willamette University data management services for relevant information.

Data Management Plans will be submitted through Fastlane as a supplementary documents; Fastlane will not accept proposals that lack Data Management Plans. Data Management Plans will be reviewed as part of the intellectual merit or broader impacts of the proposal, or both, as appropriate.

Creating a Data Management Plan

Topics to cover in a written plan include the following.

  1. Description of the project: purpose of the research, organizations and staff involved.

  2. Description of data to be collected, method of collection, its nature and format.

    As you consider this topic, you may find it helpful to consult this set of questions from MIT. The type and quantity of data produced, the audience, the frequency of changes or updates and the length of time data will be retained all affect your data plan -- and the degree to which University or external data services might contribute to the project.

  3. name of the persons responsible for data management, during your research project and over time; how you will ensure adherence to the plan over time

    If you use the Academic Commons, you may list the Academic Commons Coordinator as one of the responsible parties in your long-term data management plan.

  6. description of your short-term and long-term storage plans, including backup procedures

    Short-term storage and backup can be managed by an individual or team. Long-term storage, over a period of years or decades, is a more difficult problem that usually requires support from the university or other organization.

    For a quick overview, see Storage and Backup considerations.

    Note that ideally, three backup copies of data are maintained: the original, a second external copy located on campus, and a third copy off-site. Willamette University options include:

    • External storage media connected to a PC or other device. This is often a good solution for short-term storage and backup. Willamette University staff can advise and support researchers who use this option.
    • Data stored on Willamette University's storage area network (SAN) via mapped network drive (typically your home directory or H drive). This data is automatically backed up to magnetic tape and retained for a 3-month period at an off-site storage facility. Networked storage is a limited resource.
    • Data deposited in the Academic Commons. This data resides on Willamette University's storage area network (SAN). Backup tapes are subsequently moved to an off-site storage facility. The University's data management strategy includes permanent backup of all data in the Academic Commons.

    Beyond the university, a subject-specific repository or journal publisher Web site may be a good long-term storage strategy for some data.

  7. explanation of how you will make the data available for public use and potential secondary uses - 

    The Internet provides a variety of formal and ad hoc ways to make data available, including posting online via a project or institutional web site, submitting data to a journal publisher, or depositing data in a subject-specific data repository or local institutional repository.

    See this list of subject-specific data repositories.

    Locally, the Academic Commons DSpace repository is a good way to publish, manage, and preserve research data. DSpace is a widely adopted platform for building open digital repositories at colleges and universities. The Willamette University service provides file access controls and a third-party, persistent URL service for citing and linking to data. Access restrictions and embargo periods for data can be applied as requested by the researcher and data files easily downloaded by either authorized users or the public. DSpace records are indexed by Internet search engines and are readily discoverable.

    In addition to DSpace, an alternate digital asset management system is available through the Mark O. Hatfield Library and may be preferred in some cases.

    Contact Michael Spalti (email mspalti@willamette.edu; phone 503-370 -6356)

  8. explanation of your plans to preserve the data and ensure a long-lived format

    Changing file formats is a key consideration in preserving data. Certain formats are more likely to survive the test of time or be more easily migrated to new formats when the need arises.

    See these general guidelines for file formats from MIT.

    Locally, the Academic Commons DSpace service supports long-term access to selected file formats. Most "supported" formats are standards-based, non-proprietary and uncompressed. As such, these formats are more easily migrated to new file formats of the future. The Academic Commons list of supported formats is subject to revision.

    In addition, checksums for detecting corrupted data are routinely run for all files in the Academic Commons DSpace repository. Files will be refreshed by Hatfield Library and Willamette Integrated Technology Services staff as required.

    Contact Michael Spalti (email mspalti@willamette.edu; phone 503-370-6356)

  9. description of any arrangements that might be needed to protect participant confidentiality or intellectual property; any other legal or ethical considerations - 

    Data security: There are several factors to consider in keeping data secure. Whenever data is accessible via the Internet or the Willamette University local area network, password access controls are a minimal security requirement. Access to files in the Academic Commons can be password restricted to the PI, the project team, or opened to the public. Non-authorized users can contact the individual designated in the DSpace record to request access to the data.

    Intellectual property: Data is not copyrightable. But data licensed or otherwise obtained from third-parties may be subject to use restrictions. To make your own data explicitly available for sharing and unlimited use by others, you may wish to attach a Creative Commons CC0 waiver. When using the Academic Commons, intellectual property information can be added to the permanent record for your data.

    Confidentiality and ethical considerations: For more information about legal and ethical considerations for research involving human and non-human animal participants, consult the Willamette Institutional Review Board guidelines. When obtaining informed consent from research subjects, be sure to tell participants about how data will be stored and used and how confidentiality will be maintained.
